Meeting Julia

Julia is a young, free, open-source and promising general-purpose language, designed and developed by Jeff Bezason, Alan Edelman, Viral B. Shah and Stefan Karpinski in MIT. For years, the requirements of a language of having, on one hand, a clear, simple syntax, easy readability, and good abstraction capabilities, while in the other hand being designed for technical, high-performance and resource-intensive computations, have been thought as mutually exclusive. This led applied scientists to face the task of not only learning two different languages –one high-level, the other low-level-, but also learning how to communicate between them, which is far from trivial in a lot of cases. This is called the two language problem, and it costs programmers and scientists valuable time and effort which may be better to invest in solving the underlying true problems, which required of computations to be tackled in the first place. This is where Julia makes its appeareance, intending to bridge the gap present in the two language problem, designed from its roots for being fast and easy to understand, even for people who are not programmers or computer scientists. Julia is dynamically typed and is great for interactive use. It also uses multiple dispatch as a core design concept, which adds to the composability of the language. In conventional, single-dispatched, object-oriented programming languages, when invoking a method, one of the arguments is treated specially and it determines which of all the methods with that same name is going to be appled. Multiple dispatch is a generalization of this for all the arguments of the function called, so the method applied is going to be the one that matches exactly the number of type of the function call.

Introduction to Bayes

3.7 μs

Uncertainty is our brains' common currency. It naturally knows, for example, how plausible an event is. We know that some events, as a big volcano eruption are rare, and some others like rain are more common. This plausibility and our understanding of the world in general depends heavily on the data we measure, in other words, our past observations, and the model we have about the world. For example, we know that in the desert is less likely to rain that in a Caribbean town. We infer that because we have an internal model for such events. As we grew up, those models changed with time, we have updated them as we collected new data, had new experiences. It that sense, we are all the time interacting with new stimuli, thinking how to react and, based on past experience, what we need to do next in order to have the outcome we want.

It would be nice and useful to think about problems in that way, firstly because we can relate very much with this approach of solving problems, and also because it allows us to make models, using previous information or intuition (an arguable word for experience) and working with uncertainty in such a natural way, arising as an intrinsic property of the data and how we measure it.

A Bayesian way of thinking aligns very good with this train of thought. These groups of techniques are not new, in fact they even were the first conceptions of what we call now probability and statistics, formulated mathematically speaking by Thomas Bayes and published posthumously in 1763.

But before explicitly digging into a Bayesian framework and explaining how to express those ideas in a more mathematical way, we are going to introduce some concepts first that will help us to a better and deep understanding.

Probability distributions

3.9 μs

Histograms

We have talked about events, but how are they related to probability? A probability distribution is a function that maps every possible event in our 'event space' to a probability, a number which value is between 0 and 1. In other words, it tells us how likely is an event to occur. Events with a probability near to 0 are less likely to occur, on the contrary, events near to 1 are more likely to happen. Probability distributions may have very different and interesting properties and shapes, they can express the probability of continuous events, such as height or amount of rainfall, but also they can map discrete events, such as the number of heads in N flips of a coin, to its probability.

Histograms can be interpreted as probability distributions. The reason to this is because we have registered some total number N of events that happened in some time interval (in this case, one month) and we grouped the number of times each one ocurred. In this line of reasoning, events that happened most are more likely to happen, and hence we can say they have a higher probability associated to them. Something important to consider about histograms when dealing with a continuous variable such as, in our case, milimeters of monthly rainfall, are bins and bin size. When working with such continuous variables, the domain in which our data expresses itself (in this case, from 0 mm to approximately 450 mm) is divided in discrete intervals. In this way, given a bin size of 20mm, when constructing our histogram we have to ask 'how many rainy days have given us a precipitation measurement between 100mm and 120mm?', and then we register that number in that bin. This process is repeated for all bins to obtain our histogram. What we should do now is to normalize our histogram to have the frequency values constrained. Normalizing is just the action of adjusting the scale of variables, without changing the relative values of our data. Below we show the normalized histogram. You will notice that the frequency values are very low now. The reason for this is that when normalizing, we impose to our histogram data that the sum of the counts of all our events (or, thinking graphically, the total area of the histogram) must be 1. As probability tell us how plausible is an event, if we take into account all the events, we expect that the probability of all those events to be the maximum value, and that value is set up to 1 by convention. In that way we can compare plausibilities across different events, therefore when we say that some event has a probability of 0.6 to occur, for any event it means the same, no matter if we are talking about the probability of raining or the probability of being hit by a car. Now that you hopefully have some intuitive understanding of probability, let's dig a bit deeper into the different visiones of probability, and more specifically the one we will be focusing in this book, the Bayesian interpretation.

There exist two main approachs to probability, the frequentist approach and the one we have already introduced, the Bayesian one. The frequentist interpretation views probability as the frequency of events when a big number of repetitions are carried out. The typical example is the flip of a coin (although we will later be considering this from a Bayesian approach). To know the probability of obtaining heads when flipping a coin, a radical frequentist will tell you to perform an experiment with a lot of coint tosses (say, 1000 repetitions). When each flip is performed, you register if the outcome is heads or tails. Later, you calculate the ratio between the counts you got for heads over the total amount of repetitions. This will be your probability from a pure frequentist point of view. The Bayesian point of view has a more general interpretation. It is not bound to the frequency of events. Probability is thought as a degree of uncertainty about the event happening. It also takes into account our actual state of knowledge about that event. In a Bayesian way of thinking one could assing (and actually it is done extensively) a probability to events such as the election of some politician, while in the frequentist view this would make no sense, since we can't make large repetitions of the election to know the frequency underlying that event.

7.7 μs

Ok, but how does this Bayesian probability work? What is the hidden mechanism in it?

The first thing we should talk about is the famous Bayes' theorem. Although it not only applies for the Bayesian interpretation of probability, it is the foundation upon which the Bayesian framwork is built.

The theorem goes like this:

P(A|B)=P(B|A)P(A)P(B)

In this equation, A and B are events. They could represent any event you can assign with some probability. P(A) and P(B) are just the probabilities of events A and B, respectively, while P(A|B) and P(B|A) are called conditional probabilities. What this means, for example, in the case of P(A|B), is the probability of event A given that we know event B occurred. Think about the rolling of a dice. If I roll a dice and I ask you what is the probability of obtaining 2, you will answer that 1/6. But if I peep the dice before you see the answer and I tell you that the number is prime, what is the probability of obtaining 3? Now the probability is different, because the space of possible events is reduced. The possibilities are now 1, 2, 3, and 5, so this gives us a probability of 1/4 of obtaining 3. This is the way that conditional probabilities work.

This theorem gives us a great tool for dealing with our current degree of belief and a methodical way to update it. In this framwork, P(A) is called the prior probability of A, P(A|B) the posterior probability of A, and P(B|A) the likelihood. The probability of B in the denominator is mainly a normalization factor to make sure our posterior probability adds up to 1. It won't be so important in our stream of thought so don't worry too much about it. Bayes' theorem gives us an iterative way to update our beliefs. The prior is our belief that an event A can happen, before we have any new information. The likelihood takes into account new information B we collect, to finally obtain an updated probability for our event A. What is nice about this framwork is that we always account for the uncertainty of the world. We start with some probability and end with another probability, but unceartainty is always present. This is what real life is about.

Bayes' theorem can be extended easily not only to account for point estimates of probabilities, but also for entire probability distributions, and it is actually this way it is used to make statistical inference and predictions. Don't worry if this is too much for you right now, in the next chapter we will work on some examples to make this ideas more tangible.
